Welcome to the Complete Exercise Guide

This guide brings together 152 exercises organized by muscle group, each with photos of the starting and finishing positions, step-by-step instructions, and quick access to demonstration videos. It's built to be your go-to training reference — at home or in the gym, from your very first workout to the advanced level.

How to use this guide

1. Find the exercise. The chapters follow the classic training-split order: chest, back, shoulders, arms, core, legs, calves, cardio, and neck. Use the table of contents to jump straight to the muscle group you want.

2. Read the exercise profile. Every exercise lists the primary muscle, the supporting muscles, the equipment needed, the difficulty level, and the movement type (compound or isolation). Beginners should prioritize exercises marked Beginner and Compound — they deliver the most results for the time you put in.

3. Study the photos and the steps. The two photos show the start and the end of the movement. Read the numbered steps before your first rep, and review them any time you feel your technique start to slip.

4. Watch the video whenever you're unsure. Every exercise has a QR code: point your phone's camera at it and you'll see video demonstrations on YouTube. Seeing the full movement at real speed is the perfect complement to the photos.

5. Build (or follow) a routine. The Ready-Made Routines chapter includes 6 complete programs — for training at home or in the gym, from beginner to intermediate. If you're just starting out, don't wing it: follow a ready-made routine for 8 to 12 weeks.

Who this guide is for

  • Anyone training at home with little or no equipment (there are dozens of bodyweight-only exercises);
  • Anyone training in the gym who wants to safely mix up their stimulus;
  • Anyone just getting started who needs clear, visual instructions;
  • Anyone who already trains and wants a quick reference encyclopedia.
Important notice: this material is educational and does not replace individualized guidance from a certified fitness professional or physician. If you have any health condition, a previous injury, or persistent pain, consult a professional before beginning any exercise program.

Training Principles — the minimum you need to know

You don't need complicated formulas to get results. You need consistency applied to a handful of principles. Here they are:

Sets and reps

  • Strength: 3 to 5 sets of 3 to 6 reps, heavy loads, long rest.
  • Hypertrophy (muscle gain): 3 to 4 sets of 6 to 12 reps, challenging loads, moderate rest.
  • Muscular endurance: 2 to 3 sets of 15 to 20 reps, light to moderate loads, short rest.

If your goal is aesthetics and general health, the 8 to 12 rep range is the best place to start: the last rep of each set should be tough, but doable with good technique.

Rest between sets

  • Heavy compound lifts (squat, deadlift, bench press): 2 to 3 minutes;
  • Isolation exercises (curls, lateral raises, leg extensions): 60 to 90 seconds;
  • Circuits and cardio: 30 to 60 seconds, or as the protocol calls for.

Progressive overload — the golden rule

Muscle only grows if the stimulus increases over time. The simplest way to progress:

  1. Pick a rep range (e.g., 8 to 12);
  2. Once you can complete every set at the top of the range with good technique, add weight — 2 to 5%;
  3. The heavier load will drop you back to the bottom of the range (8 reps). Repeat the cycle.

With bodyweight exercises, progress by changing the variation (e.g., incline push-up → standard push-up → decline push-up) or by adding reps and sets.

Frequency and weekly volume

  • Each muscle group responds well to 10 to 20 sets per week, spread across 2 or more sessions;
  • Training a muscle twice a week beats cramming everything into a single day;
  • More training isn't always better: past a certain volume, all you're doing is piling up fatigue.

Technique before load

One clean rep with a moderate load is worth more than three cheated reps with a heavy one. Signs the weight is too heavy: your range of motion is shrinking, your torso is swinging for momentum, you're losing control on the way down. Drop the load and master the movement — the photos and steps for every exercise in this guide exist for exactly that reason.

Tempo and range of motion

  • Lower the weight under control (2 to 3 seconds on the eccentric) and drive it up with intent;
  • Use the fullest range of motion your mobility allows safely — full range creates more stimulus per rep.

Rest and recovery

Muscle grows outside the gym. Sleep 7 to 9 hours, keep your protein intake steady throughout the day (a practical target: 1.6 to 2.2 g per kg of body weight), and give a muscle group at least 48 hours before training it heavy again.

Warm-Up and Safety

The 3-Layer Warm-Up (8 to 12 minutes)

1. Raise your temperature (3–5 min). Any light, continuous activity works: jump rope, treadmill, bike, jumping jacks. The goal is to break a light sweat and get your heart rate up.

2. Targeted mobility (3–4 min). Big, controlled movements through the joints you'll be training that day: arm and wrist circles, trunk rotations, slow bodyweight squats sinking down under control, leg swings.

3. Warm-up sets (2–3 min). On the first exercise of your workout, do 1 to 2 light sets before your working sets: roughly 50%, then 70% of your working weight, with fewer reps. This rehearses the movement pattern and primes your nervous system.

Long static stretching (holding a stretch for 30+ seconds) works best after your workout or in separate sessions — before training, stick with dynamic mobility.

Safety Rules That Prevent 90% of Injuries

  1. Learn the movement before you load it. First time doing an exercise? Perform it with just your bodyweight or a minimal load until the pattern feels natural.
  2. A neutral spine is non-negotiable on squats, deadlifts, bent over rows, and good mornings. If your lower back rounds, the weight is too heavy or your mobility doesn't yet allow the range you're using.
  3. Don't train through pain. Muscle soreness is expected; joint pain, sharp twinges, or pain that radiates is not. Stop the exercise, and if it persists, get it checked out by a professional.
  4. Use collars and safeties. Clips on the bar, safety pins in the rack, a stable bench. On challenging loads for the bench press and squat, use a spotter or set the safeties in the rack.
  5. Breathe with a pattern. Simple rule: exhale on the effort (the way up), inhale on the way down. For maximal loads, the breath-holding technique (Valsalva) should be learned under supervision.
  6. Progress one variable at a time. Don't increase load, volume, and frequency all in the same week.
  7. Stay hydrated and fueled. Training deep in a fasted or dehydrated state wrecks your technique and performance.

Signs You Should Stop Your Workout

  • Dizziness, nausea, or blurred vision;
  • Sharp, localized pain in a joint or tendon;
  • Sudden loss of strength on one side of your body;
  • Irregular heartbeat or shortness of breath out of proportion to the effort.

Safety isn't a brake on your results — it's what lets you train without interruption for years. And consistency is the only "secret" that actually exists.

Ready-Made Routines

Six complete programs built from the exercises in this guide. Pick one and run it for 8 to 12 weeks before switching. For all of them: warm up first (previous chapter), rest 60–180 s between sets depending on the exercise, and apply the load progression from the principles chapter.

Routine 2 — Home, Intermediate (dumbbells, ABC 3–6x/week)

A pair of adjustable dumbbells covers everything.

Workout A — Push: Dumbbell Bench Press 4×8–12 · Standing Dumbbell Shoulder Press 3×8–12 · Flat Dumbbell Flyes 3×10–15 · Dumbbell Overhead Triceps Extension 3×10–15 · Close-Grip (Diamond) Pushups 2× max.

Workout B — Pull: One-Arm Dumbbell Row 4×8–12 · Bent Over Dumbbell Row 3×8–12 · Reverse Dumbbell Flyes 3×12–15 · Alternating Dumbbell Curls 3×10–12 · Hammer Curls 2×10–12.

Workout C — Legs and Core: Goblet Squat 4×8–12 · Dumbbell Lunges 3×10 per leg · Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift 3×10–12 · Standing Dumbbell Calf Raises 3×15–20 · Crunches 3×15 · Side Plank 2×20–30 s per side.

Cycle A → B → C and start over. 3 days/week = each workout once; 6 days = each workout twice.

Routine 4 — Gym, Intermediate (classic ABC split, 4–6x/week)

A — Chest, Shoulders, and Triceps: Barbell Bench Press 4×6–10 · Incline Dumbbell Press 3×8–12 · Machine Chest Fly 3×10–15 · Seated Dumbbell Shoulder Press 3×8–12 · Side Lateral Raise 3×12–15 · Rope Triceps Pushdown 3×10–15.

B — Back, Traps, and Biceps: Pullups (or Lat Pulldown) 4×6–10 · Bent Over Barbell Row 3×8–12 · Seated Cable Row 3×8–12 · Face Pull 3×12–15 · Barbell Curl 3×8–12 · Cable Rope Hammer Curl 2×10–12.

C — Full Legs: Barbell Squat 4×6–10 · Leg Press 3×10–12 · Romanian Deadlift 3×8–12 · Leg Extension 2×12–15 · Seated Leg Curl 2×12–15 · Standing Machine Calf Raise 4×12–20.

Routine 5 — Fat Loss and Conditioning (circuit, 3–4x/week)

Circuit: run through all 6 exercises back to back, 30–45 s of work and 15–30 s to transition; rest 2 min and repeat for 3 to 4 rounds.

  1. Jump Squats (or light Bodyweight Squats)
  2. Pushups (any variation)
  3. Mountain Climbers
  4. Bent Over Dumbbell Row (light load)
  5. Bicycle Crunches (air bike)
  6. Jump Rope (or running in place)

Finish with 10–20 min of light steady-state cardio (treadmill, bike, elliptical). Pair it with a moderate calorie deficit — training doesn't make up for diet.

Routine 6 — Basic Strength (barbell, 3x/week, alternating A/B)

Workout A: Barbell Squat 5×5 · Barbell Bench Press 5×5 · Bent Over Barbell Row 5×5.

Workout B: Barbell Squat 5×5 · Standing Military Press 5×5 · Barbell Deadlift 1×5 (one heavy top set).

Alternate A and B (week 1: A-B-A; week 2: B-A-B). Start with comfortable weights and add 5 lb (2.5 kg) per session as long as you can complete all the reps — when you stall 3 sessions in a row, drop 10% and build back up.

How to Choose

  • Never trained before: Routine 1 (home) or 3 (gym).
  • Been training 6+ months: Routine 2 (home) or 4 (gym).
  • Main goal is fat loss: Routine 5, with daily walks on your off days.
  • Want to get strong on the basics: Routine 6.

Step by step
